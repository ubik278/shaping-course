The Shaping Course

Stop translating.Start shaping.

An eight-session cohort that moves product designers from making the screens to shaping what gets built — the problem framing, evidence, and influence that sit before and around the design work.

8 sessions Bi-weekly Cohort of 5 One live project, start to finish
Why this, why now

AI made the screens cheap. It made judgement expensive.

AI tools now produce workable screens and prototypes in minutes. What they can't do is understand the problem, weigh the evidence, make the call, and win the room. That's where a designer's value is moving — and it's what this course trains.

The pattern we're changing

A request arrives and the design tool opens the same day. Every request is treated the same, urgent or not. Decisions get made elsewhere, and design executes them.

It feels productive. But beautiful execution of the wrong brief is the most expensive thing a team can produce.

What we're building toward

Designers who question the brief before building, bring evidence instead of opinions, manage their own priorities, and present work so a room can decide.

Less waiting for instructions. More ownership of what's worth building.

How it works

Practice, not theory.

Everyone already knows they should "understand the problem first." Knowing isn't the gap — practice is. So the course runs like training: one skill per session, applied immediately to real work, results presented to the group next time.

01

One concept per session

A short pre-read, a focused walkthrough, then straight into applying it. Nothing taught that isn't used within the fortnight.

02

Homework is your real work

No artificial exercises. Each participant carries one live project through all eight sessions — the practice happens inside it.

03

You present every time

Five minutes, a fixed structure, eight sessions running. Explaining your thinking to a room stops being an event and becomes a habit.

The eight sessions

The arc — upstream to downstream.

Each session builds on the last, following the shape of a real project from first question to final pitch.

01

Understand the problem

Answer four questions — what problem, for whom, why it matters, how we'll know it worked — before any design work begins.

AfterRequests get questioned before they get designed.
02

Understand your stakeholders

Learn how your PM is measured, why briefs arrive solution-shaped, and how to disagree in a way that helps rather than obstructs.

AfterProposals are argued in the language of business goals, not design preference.
03

Rough before polished

Match how finished the work looks to how certain the direction is. Early and uncertain means rough on purpose.

AfterFeedback arrives earlier, when it's still cheap to act on.
04

Test with real users

Fast, lightweight usability testing — five people, twenty minutes, one task — turned into three plain findings.

AfterDesign discussions run on evidence, not opinion.
05

Prioritise & recommend

Run a visible, shared priority list, and bring options with a recommendation instead of open questions.

After"What do you think?" becomes "I recommend this, and here's why."
06

Use AI in your workflow

Let AI handle the grunt work — synthesis, first drafts, variations, test prototypes — while the judgement stays with you.

AfterAI is a tool in the workflow, not a threat outside it.
07

Explore before you commit

Generate several genuinely different directions — and pick one with a reason — instead of polishing the first idea.

AfterThree real options on the table before you commit to one.
08

Present & convince

Present a project as a case — problem, evidence, recommendation, a clear ask — first to the cohort, then to real stakeholders.

AfterPresentations end with a decision, not with "thoughts?"
The transformation

A different way of working — visible to others.

The aim isn't a certificate. It's a shift in how the work gets done that the people around you notice first: better questions, earlier involvement, clearer recommendations, faster decisions.

We measure it, we don't guess. Before the first session and after the last, each participant's main PM answers three short questions about how the designer works. The difference is the result — with a follow-up thirty days later to see which habits held.

In short

The screens were never the point.

Eight sessions, one hour every two weeks, applied to the work already on your plate. The same designer — operating one level higher, where the decisions get made and the tools can't reach.
